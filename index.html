Step 3 — Choose your payoff strategy
Avalanche
Mathematically optimal
How it works: Pay minimums on all debts, then throw every extra dollar at the highest interest rate debt first.

✓ Pros: Saves the most money in total interest. Fastest path to debt freedom in dollars. Best for high-rate debts like credit cards.

✗ Cons: Your highest-rate debt may also be a large balance — it can take months before you see a debt fully paid off, which can feel discouraging.

Best for: People who are motivated by numbers and can stay disciplined without quick wins.
Snowball
Psychologically powerful
How it works: Pay minimums on all debts, then attack the smallest balance first regardless of rate.

✓ Pros: Quick wins build momentum. Eliminating accounts reduces financial stress and complexity. Research shows higher completion rates than avalanche.

✗ Cons: Usually costs more in total interest — sometimes significantly if small debts have low rates.

Best for: People who need motivation, have struggled with debt payoff before, or carry many small accounts.
Hybrid
Balanced approach
How it works: Ranks debts by a blended score combining interest rate AND balance size. Prioritizes high-rate, lower-balance debts first.

✓ Pros: Gets early wins like snowball while keeping interest costs closer to avalanche. Adapts to your actual debt mix.

✗ Cons: Not perfectly optimal mathematically, not purely psychological — a compromise on both.

Best for: Most people. Especially when debt balances and rates are mixed across multiple accounts.
📋 Planning Checklist — What Else To Consider ▼
Tax strategy
Roth conversions during low-income years before RMDs (age 73)
Medicare / IRMAA
Surcharges kick in at $206k MAGI — plan conversions 2 yrs ahead
Sequence of returns
First 5 years of retirement carry the most risk — consider a 1–2 yr cash buffer
Long-term care
~70% of people 65+ need LTC. Average cost: $5k–$10k/month — insure or self-fund
Life insurance
Term coverage during peak earning and debt years — review at retirement
Estate planning
Will, POA, healthcare directive, and updated beneficiary designations
Emergency fund
3–6 months liquid even in retirement — never tap investments for surprises
HSA strategy
Triple tax advantage — invest HSA and preserve for healthcare in retirement
Catch-up contributions
Age 50+: extra $7,500 to 401k and $1,000 to IRA annually
Bond glide path
Increase bond allocation as retirement approaches to reduce sequence risk
Withdrawal order
Taxable → Tax-deferred → Roth — sequence minimizes lifetime tax bill
Part-time income
Even $1k–$2k/mo in early retirement dramatically improves MC success rate
Geographic arbitrage
Lower cost-of-living in retirement can reduce your true number by 20–30%
College funding
529s grow tax-free, reduce taxable estate, and transfer to grandkids if unused
